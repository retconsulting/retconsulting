From Prompt to Loop Engineering, a simple guide for the non-coders and knowledge workers

Prompt → Context → Harness → Loops

AIExplainer

2026-07-11 · 5 MIN READ

Prompt, context, harness, loop: four terms for how we work with AI today, each a layer built on the one before, not rival trends to choose between. Read together, they explain how an assistant went from answering a single question to carrying out a whole task on its own. Knowing where a piece of work sits on that ladder is what lets you decide what to hand to AI, how far to let it run, and where a person still needs to stay in charge. Here is the ladder from the bottom up, in plain language with no code, the way I explain it in my trainings.

01 / PROMPT ENGINEERINGYou write, it answers

Prompt engineering is the art of writing good instructions. The model does not read your mind, it reads your words. Give it a role, a clear task, the context it needs, and the shape you want the answer to take, and a rough request turns into a reliable one. Everything it needs fits in a single message.

Same task, very different results. And at this level the model works only from what it already knows. Ask it to explain the difference between gross and net margin, or to soften a blunt message into something you can send, and it produces the answer on its own. No search, no tools, no documents: one question, one answer. That is prompt engineering, the foundation everything else is built on.

02 / CONTEXT ENGINEERINGIt fetches what it needs

Your prompt does not sit in there alone. It lands in the model's context window, its working memory for this exchange, next to everything else the model is holding at that moment: the instructions it was given, the conversation up to now, any documents you have added, and whatever it has just looked up. The window is roomy, but its size is fixed, so all of it competes for the same space.

What makes this powerful is that the assistant fills that space itself, on demand: it opens your files, queries a database, searches the web, calls your business tools. Ask what your main competitor announced this week and a prompt alone cannot answer, because the model's knowledge stops at its training date. So it searches, reads and selects what matters before it replies. That is context engineering.

Notice what just appeared: a loop. Search, read, decide whether that is enough, search again. The assistant runs it on its own, until it judges the context sufficient.

03 / HARNESS ENGINEERINGSomeone keeps the plan

Context has a hard limit: the window fills up. On any job longer than a few minutes, the assistant runs out of room and starts compressing its own memory to make space. Details leak at every pass, like a colleague taking notes of their notes until the nuance is gone.

The fix is to run the work from the outside. A harness is the software wrapped around the assistant that does exactly this. It holds the plan, the to-do list and the progress in its own memory, outside the window. It hands the assistant one small step at a time with only the information that step needs, takes back the result, ticks it off, and moves to the next. The assistant never has to hold the whole job in its head.

Think of turn-by-turn directions. You never hold the whole route in your head: the GPS keeps it, gives you one turn at a time, and always knows where you are and what comes next. Ask an agent to rebuild a large website end to end, and the harness is that GPS, taking the model from the first step to the last without ever losing its place.

Second loop, one level up: task after task, until the list is empty.

04 / LOOP ENGINEERINGNobody presses the button

Look back at those three levels. Each one still starts with a human typing a request. Loop engineering removes that last dependency: the system prompts itself, on a schedule, on an event, on a detected problem.

A concrete picture. Think of the report you rebuild every week: pull the latest figures from a few tools, reconcile them, flag anything odd, and write a short summary for the team. A loop runs that whole chain on its own, on schedule. It gathers the data, compares it with last week, drafts the summary, and leaves only the exceptions for you to judge. If a source is missing, it retries or flags it and moves on. Repeat that across the small operations a business runs every day: onboarding checks, invoice sorting, weekly competitor scans. The gain is not a faster answer, it is work that no longer waits on you to begin. You review the outcome, not the keystrokes.

Running that reliably takes a few moving parts, each with one job:

  • AutomationRuns tasks on a schedule or trigger.
  • WorkspacesIsolated, so parallel changes never collide.
  • SkillsReusable know-how the system draws on.
  • ConnectorsLinks the system to your business tools.
  • SubagentsSeparate checks that verify finished work.
  • StateProgress that survives between separate runs.

ANATOMYWhat every loop needs

You have now seen three loops in this guide: an assistant that searches until it has enough, a harness that works through a task list, an operation that rebuilds a report on its own each week. They do different jobs, yet every dependable loop is built from the same five parts, on the one running cycle below. Miss one, and the loop misbehaves.

It starts with a trigger: what sets each run going. Instead of waiting for you to ask, a loop starts on a schedule, on an event like a new invoice arriving, or on a problem it detects. That is the shift this whole idea rests on: work that begins without you.

It also needs a goal, a target and a clear definition of done: send the weekly summary, make the tests pass, answer the question. Without a goal it cannot tell progress from motion, and it never knows when to stop.

Then it needs feedback: after each pass, the loop compares what it produced against that goal. This one choice matters more than any other. A closed loop checks its own result and corrects course, like a thermostat that keeps reading the room and nudging the heating until it reaches the temperature you set. A closed loop is self-correcting, and it is the only kind to trust with real work. An open loop follows a fixed plan and never looks back, like a sprinkler on a timer that waters the garden whether or not it rained in the night. Open loops are simpler, and they drift the moment reality stops matching the plan.

Next, a stop condition: a rule for when to end. The goal is reached, a budget of time or attempts is spent, progress has stalled, or a person calls a halt. This is the brake. A loop without one does not keep working in a useful way, it just keeps spending: time, money, and your patience, while the output turns to noise. Most of the worry about these systems is really the worry about a loop with no brake.

Last, guardrails. Because the loop runs while you are not watching, you decide up front what it may touch, how far it may reach, and which actions need a human to approve them first. The goal says where the loop is heading; the guardrails say where it must never go.

What this changes for you

Loop engineering is still early. So far, no loop has produced what the layers beneath it could not, and a self-prompting system left unchecked produces mostly volume, rarely value. The progression underneath it, though, is well established, and every layer keeps its weight: a badly written prompt stays badly written inside the most sophisticated loop. What widens is the scope of what can be delegated, from an answer, to a task, to a project, to an operation that runs on its own.

You already practise prompt engineering every day, often without naming it. The next skill is not writing better requests, but designing the checks around work that runs without you: what runs on a schedule, what gets verified and by what, and where a person has to stay in the loop. That is a product skill more than a technical one, and it can be learnt.
