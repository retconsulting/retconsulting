From Prompt to Loop Engineering, a simple guide for the non-coders and knowledge workers

Prompt → Context → Harness → Loops

AIExplainer

2026-07-11 · 7 MIN READ

Prompt engineering, context engineering, harness engineering, loop engineering. The industry coins a new one every few months, each sounding more like jargon than the last. Hype, or something you actually need to learn? The twist: they are not four rival trends, but four rungs of one ladder, each built on the one below. Together they trace how an assistant went from answering a single question to carrying out a whole task on its own. Never heard of any of them? Good, that is exactly who this is for. Here is that ladder from the bottom up, in plain language with no code, the way I explain it in my trainings, so you leave knowing what to hand to AI, how far to let it run, and where a person still needs to stay in charge.

01 / PROMPT ENGINEERINGYou write, it answers

Prompt engineering is the art of writing good instructions. The model does not read your mind, it reads your words. Give it a role, a clear task, the context it needs, the limits to keep to, and the shape you want the answer to take, and a rough request turns into a reliable one. Everything it needs fits in a single message.

Same task, very different results. And at this level the model works only from what it already knows. Ask it to explain the difference between gross and net margin, or to soften a blunt message into something you can send, and it produces the answer on its own. No search, no tools, no documents: one question, one answer. That is prompt engineering, the foundation everything else is built on.

02 / CONTEXT ENGINEERINGIt fetches what it needs

Your prompt does not arrive alone. It lands in the model's context window, its working memory for this exchange, next to everything else the model is holding at that moment: the instructions it was given, the conversation up to now, any documents you have added, and whatever it has just looked up. The window is roomy, but its size is fixed, so all of it competes for the same space.

What makes this powerful is that the assistant fills that space itself, on demand: it opens your files, queries a database, searches the web, calls your business tools. Ask what your main competitor announced this week and a prompt alone cannot answer, because the model's knowledge stops at its knowledge cutoff. So it searches, reads and selects what matters before it replies. That is context engineering.

Notice what just appeared: a loop. Search, read, decide whether that is enough, search again. The assistant runs it on its own, until it judges the context sufficient.

03 / HARNESS ENGINEERINGSomeone keeps the plan

Context has a hard limit: the window only holds so much. Give the assistant a big enough job and it runs out of room, then starts compressing its own memory to make space. Details leak at every pass, like a colleague taking notes of their notes until the nuance is gone.

The fix is to run the work from the outside. A harness is the software wrapped around the assistant that does exactly this. It holds the plan, the to-do list and the progress in its own memory, outside the window. It hands the assistant one small step at a time with only the information that step needs, takes back the result, ticks it off, and moves to the next. The assistant never has to hold the whole job in its head.

Think of turn-by-turn directions. You never hold the whole route in your head: the GPS keeps it, gives you one turn at a time, and always knows where you are and what comes next. Ask an agent to rebuild a large website, or to turn a folder of scattered notes into one clean handbook, and the harness is that GPS, taking the model from the first step to the last without ever losing its place.

Second loop, one level up: task after task, until the list is empty.

04 / LOOP ENGINEERINGNobody presses the button

Look back at those three levels. Each one still starts with a person typing a request. Loop engineering removes that last dependency: the agent prompts itself. The change is one of stance. Instead of treating AI as a chatbot you talk to, you write it something closer to a job description, a set of instructions, a trigger that starts the work, and the tools to carry it out. Once it fires, the agent runs on its own, checks its progress, and decides the next step until the job is done.

A concrete picture: the report you rebuild every week. Pull the latest figures from a few tools, reconcile them, flag anything odd, write a short summary for the team. A loop runs that whole chain on schedule, gathers the data, compares it with last week, drafts the summary, and leaves only the exceptions for you to judge. Repeat it across the small operations a business runs every day, and the gain is not a faster answer, it is work that no longer waits on you to begin. You review the outcome, not the keystrokes.

The first thing you design is the trigger, what makes the agent start a run. Three come from outside it; the fourth is the loop restarting itself until a goal is met.

Left to run on its own, a loop can make a mess quickly. Working safely takes a specific setup, each part with one job.

  • WorkspacesIsolated sandboxes, so it never overwrites human work or collides with another agent.
  • SkillsStandard, repeatable methods for common tasks, so it does not reinvent one each time.
  • ConnectionsThe integrations that let it read your files, reach your tools, and send messages.
  • StateA running memory of where it is, so it does not go round in circles.
  • Sub-agentsTemporary helpers it spins up for a sub-task, without derailing the main job.

The real power shows when loops stack. They are recursive: a scheduled loop can wake up, look at a large piece of work, and start several goal-driven loops for different parts of it. The agent becomes a manager, handing work to a temporary team of its own and waiting for each to report back: a loop that runs loops.

There is a catch. When you work with a model by hand, you can stop it the moment it drifts. A loop runs while you are not watching, so you cannot reach for the brakes, and a goal-driven loop judges its own success. Give it a vague definition of done and it will not know when to stop: it keeps guessing, failing, and looping, running up the bill until you step in. Precision is the price of automation.

ANATOMYWhat every loop needs

You have now seen three loops in this guide: an assistant that searches until it has enough, a harness that works through a task list, an operation that rebuilds a report on its own each week. They do different jobs, yet every dependable loop is built from the same five parts, on the one running cycle below. Miss one, and the loop misbehaves.

It starts with a trigger: the reason each run begins, one of the four kinds above. Without one, the loop just waits for you to ask, the very dependence loop engineering set out to remove.

It also needs a goal, a target and a clear definition of done: send the weekly summary, make the tests pass, answer the question. Without a goal it cannot tell progress from motion, and it never knows when to stop.

Then it needs feedback: after each pass, the loop compares what it produced against that goal. This one choice matters more than any other. A closed loop checks its own result and corrects course, like a thermostat that keeps reading the room and nudging the heating until it reaches the temperature you set. A closed loop is self-correcting, and it is the only kind to trust with real work. An open loop follows a fixed plan and never looks back, like a sprinkler on a timer that waters the garden whether or not it rained in the night. Open loops are simpler, and they drift the moment reality stops matching the plan.

Next, a stop condition: a rule for when to end. The goal is reached, a budget of time or attempts is spent, progress has stalled, or a person calls a halt. This is the brake, and after the last section you know why it matters: a loop that cannot stop itself is the one that runs away. Most of the worry about these systems is really the worry about a loop with no brake.

Last, guardrails. Because the loop runs while you are not watching, you decide up front what it may touch, how far it may reach, and which actions need a human to approve them first. The goal says where the loop is heading; the guardrails say where it must never go.

What this changes for you

Prompt, context, harness, loop: one ladder, not four trends to choose between. The power is real: climb a rung and the work starts running without you, the scope widening as you go, from a single answer, to a task, to a project, to an operation that runs on its own. You stop trading your time for output and start reviewing results instead.

The limit is just as real. A loop only amplifies what sits beneath it, so a weak prompt stays weak inside the most advanced one, and a vague definition of done sends it circling, burning budget for nothing. Because it runs while you are away, precision and a few human checks are the whole game. Knowing what to hand over, and where to keep a hand on it, is a product skill more than a technical one, and it can be learnt.
