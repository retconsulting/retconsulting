From Prompt to Loop Engineering, a simple guide for the non-coders and knowledge workers

Prompt → Context → Harness → Loops

AIExplainer

2026-07-11 · 8 MIN READ

As AI becomes everyone's tool, it brings a flood of new concepts to understand and, more importantly, to apply. From prompt engineering to context engineering, and from harness engineering to loop engineering, this guide explains what each one means, how they build on one another, and how to get the most out of AI on your hardest tasks, whether you write code or work with knowledge.

01 / PROMPT ENGINEERINGThe baseline

What it is

Prompt engineering is simply the craft of writing good instructions for an AI model. A model can't read our minds (yet): it only works from the exact words we give it, so the more deliberately we frame a request, the more reliable the answer becomes.

How it works

Give it a role to play, a clear task, the context it needs, the limits to respect, and the shape the answer should take, and a vague ask turns into a dependable one, as the two versions below show. Everything the model needs still fits inside that single message, with no outside help required.

Example

The task in the figure above is the same in both versions, yet the results are worlds apart. Ask the model to explain gross versus net margin, or to work out how many cheeseburgers would fit between the Earth and the Moon, and it answers on its own: no search, no tools, just its own reasoning.

Limitations

This is where prompt engineering hits its ceiling. On its own, the model can't fetch new information, check today's numbers, or verify its own answer: everything has to already be in the message, or already in its training. That's the foundation everything else in this guide builds on.

02 / CONTEXT ENGINEERINGAdding autonomy

What it is

Our prompt never arrives alone, though. It lands inside the model's context window, the working memory it keeps for this one exchange. Everything else it is holding at that moment shares that same space: the instructions it was given, the conversation so far, any documents we have added, and whatever it has just looked up.

How it works

That window is generous, but its size is fixed, so every one of those pieces is competing for the same limited room, as the breakdown below shows. What makes context engineering powerful is that the assistant fills that space itself, without being told exactly how: it opens our files, queries a database, searches the web, or calls whichever business tool we have connected.

Example

Take a simple example. Ask what a competitor announced this week, and a prompt on its own can only guess, because the model's knowledge stops at its knowledge cutoff, a fixed point in time. Give it the autonomy to search instead, and it goes and looks, reads what it finds, keeps only what matters, and only then replies. That back-and-forth of searching, reading and judging what to keep is context engineering.

Notice what just appeared: a loop. Search, read, judge whether that is enough, then search again, a cycle the assistant runs on its own until it decides it has gathered enough context.

Limitations

Context comes with a hard limit, though: the window only holds so much. Give the assistant a big enough job and it eventually runs out of room, then starts compressing its own memory to free up space. A little detail leaks at every pass, like a colleague taking notes of their notes until the nuance is gone.

03 / HARNESS ENGINEERINGManaging from the outside

What it is

The fix is to run the work from the outside, instead of trying to cram it all into one exchange. Think of the AI model itself as the engine of a car: powerful on its own, but not much use for actually getting anywhere without a steering wheel, seats, wheels and a body built around it. A harness is that surrounding structure: the software wrapped around the assistant that keeps it pointed at the job. Claude Code, Cursor and GitHub Copilot's agent mode are everyday examples, each one the same kind of model wrapped in different plumbing for a different job.

How it works

It keeps the plan, the to-do list and the progress in its own memory, outside the window, and hands the assistant one small step at a time, with only the information that step needs. It takes back the result, ticks it off, and moves on to the next, so the assistant never has to hold the whole job in its head.

Example

That same idea plays out step by step, the way turn-by-turn directions do. We never hold the whole route in our head: the GPS keeps it for us, gives us one turn at a time, and always knows where we are and what comes next. Ask an agent to rebuild a large website, or to turn a folder of scattered notes into one clean handbook, and the harness plays exactly that role of GPS, carrying the model from the first step to the last without ever losing its place.

And there is our second loop, one level up: task after task, until the list is empty.

Limitations

A harness still waits for us. It can carry a job from the first step to the last without losing the plan, but someone still has to start it, point it at the work and press go: it doesn't decide what to do next on its own.

04 / LOOP ENGINEERINGAutomating the thinking

What it is

Look back at those three levels and notice that each one still starts the same way: a person types a request. Loop engineering removes that last dependency, so the agent prompts itself.

It is less a new technique than a change in how we relate to the tool: instead of treating AI as a chatbot we open and chat with, we hand it something closer to a job description, made of a set of standing instructions, a trigger that kicks off the work, and the tools to carry it out. Once that trigger fires, the agent runs on its own: it checks its own progress and decides the next step until the job is done.

How it works

The first thing we design is the trigger, what makes the agent start a run in the first place. Three of them come from outside the loop; the fourth is the loop restarting itself until a goal is met.

Left entirely to its own devices, a loop can make a mess surprisingly quickly: without the right setup underneath it, it just repeats whatever we told it, good or bad, on its own schedule. Working safely takes a specific setup, each part with one clear job:

  • WorkspacesIsolated sandboxes, so it never overwrites human work or collides with another agent.
  • SkillsStandard, repeatable methods for common tasks, so it does not reinvent one each time.
  • ConnectionsThe integrations that let it read your files, reach your tools, and send messages.
  • StateA running memory of where it is, so it does not go round in circles.
  • Sub-agentsTemporary helpers it spins up for a sub-task, without derailing the main job.

The real power shows once loops start to stack on top of each other, a skill worth naming on its own: loopcraft. Loops are recursive, meaning a loop can spin up other loops, each capable of doing the same.

Example

Take a concrete example: the report you rebuild every week. You pull the latest figures from a few tools, reconcile them, flag anything odd and write a short summary for the team.

A loop can run that whole chain on schedule, gathering the data, comparing it with last week, drafting the summary and leaving only the exceptions for you to judge.

Repeat that across the small operations a business runs every day, and the real gain isn't a faster answer, it's work that no longer waits on you to begin. You end up reviewing the outcome rather than the keystrokes.

Picture a loop built to keep a codebase healthy: on a heartbeat, it checks for bugs by itself, isolates a safe copy of the code to work in, drafts a fix, hands that fix to a second loop whose only job is to verify it, and only then opens the change for a person to approve. The agent effectively becomes a manager, delegating to a temporary team of its own and waiting for each member to report back before moving on, a loop that runs loops.

LIMITATIONSBeware the cost and time sink

When we work with a model by hand, we can stop it the moment it drifts. A loop runs while we're not watching, so we can't simply reach for the brakes, and a goal-driven loop judges its own success.

Give it a vague definition of done and it won't know when to stop: it keeps guessing, failing and looping, burning through tokens (the units its work actually costs) until someone steps in.

When the goal is genuinely unclear, or when it can't quite tell whether a change actually helped, it can also start thrashing: changing things back and forth without ever converging on a real fix.

And because a loop can ship work faster than any one person can review it line by line, it opens a second risk worth watching for, call it comprehension debt: the growing gap between what now exists in our project and what we actually understand about it.

Precision, in other words, is the price we pay for automation. A person still needs to sit in the judgment seat, confirming the result really works and approving anything sensitive or hard to undo.

ANATOMYWhat every loop needs

We have now met several loops in this guide: an assistant that searches until it has enough, a harness that works its way through a task list, and an operation that rebuilds a report on its own each week.

They do very different jobs, yet every dependable loop is built from the same five parts, shown here as a single running cycle. Miss one of them, and the loop starts to misbehave.

It starts with a trigger, the reason each run begins, one of the four kinds we just saw. Without one, the loop just sits there waiting for us to ask, which is exactly the dependence loop engineering set out to remove.

It also needs a goal, a target and a clear definition of done: send the weekly summary, make the tests pass, answer the question. Without one, it can't tell progress from motion, and it never quite knows when to stop.

Then it needs feedback: after each pass, the loop compares what it produced against that goal. This single choice matters more than any other. A closed loop checks its own result and corrects course, like a thermostat that keeps reading the room and nudging the heating until it reaches the temperature we set; it is self-correcting, and it is the only kind we'd trust with real work.

An open loop, by contrast, follows a fixed plan and never looks back, like a sprinkler on a timer that waters the garden whether or not it rained overnight. Open loops are simpler, but they drift the moment reality stops matching the plan.

Next, a stop condition, a rule for when to end: the goal is reached, a budget of time or attempts is spent, progress has stalled, or a person calls a halt. This is the brake, and we already know why it matters: a loop that can't stop itself is exactly the one that runs away.

Most of the worry around these systems really comes down to this: a loop with no brake.

Last, guardrails. Because the loop runs while we're not watching, we decide up front what it may touch, how far it may reach, and which actions need a human to approve them first. If the goal says where the loop is heading, the guardrails say where it must never go.

[ KEY_TAKEAWAY ]

What this changes for you

Prompt, context, harness, loop: it's one ladder, not four trends to choose between. The power is real. Climb a rung and the work starts running without us, the scope widening as we go, from a single answer, to a task, to a project, to an operation that runs on its own. We stop trading our time for output and start reviewing results instead.

The limit is just as real. A loop only ever amplifies what sits beneath it, so a weak prompt stays weak inside the most advanced one, and a vague definition of done sends it circling, burning budget for nothing. Because it runs while we're away, precision and a few well-placed human checks are the whole game. Knowing what to hand over, and where to keep a hand on it, is a product skill more than a technical one, and the good news is that it can be learnt.
