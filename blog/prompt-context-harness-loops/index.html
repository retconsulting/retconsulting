Prompt → Context → Harness → Loops

2026-07-11 · 5 MIN READ · PRODUCT & AI

Another quarter, another buzzword. After prompt engineering came context engineering, then harness engineering, and now the industry talks about loop engineering. Clients ask me whether they should care. Short answer: the word is new, the idea is not. Here is the whole ladder, explained the way I explain it in my trainings.

01 / PROMPTYou write, it answers

Prompt engineering is the art of writing good instructions. Tell an assistant: you are a customer service agent, stay polite, stay helpful. From then on it plays that role for every question. Everything it needs fits in a single message.

Ask it how many cheeseburgers you would need to stack to reach the Moon, and it works the answer out from what it already knows. No search, no tools, no documents. One question, one answer. That is prompt engineering, and it remains the foundation of everything that follows.

02 / CONTEXTIt fetches what it needs

Your prompt lives inside the model's context window: its working memory. And there is plenty of room left in there. The next step was to let the assistant fill that memory itself: open your files, query a database, search the web, call your business tools.

Ask what NASA discovered this week and a prompt alone cannot answer, because the model's knowledge stops at its training date. The assistant has to search, read and select before it can reply. That is context engineering.

Notice what just appeared: a loop. Search, read, decide whether that is enough, search again. The assistant runs it on its own, until it judges the context sufficient.

03 / HARNESSSomeone keeps the plan

Context has a hard limit: the window fills up. On any task longer than a few minutes, the assistant starts summarising its own memory to make room, and details leak at every pass. Like a colleague taking notes of their own notes: by the third generation, the nuance is gone.

The fix is to manage the work from outside. A harness keeps the plan, the task list and the progress state outside the window, feeds the assistant one step at a time, and ticks each step off. Ask an agent to rebuild a large, complex website end to end: the harness is what stops it choking halfway.

Second loop, one level up: task after task, until the list is empty.

04 / LOOPSNobody presses the button

Look back at those three levels. Each one still starts with a human typing a request. Loop engineering removes that last dependency: the system prompts itself, on a schedule, on an event, on a detected problem.

A concrete picture. Imagine a site that tracks World Cup scores, built once with the three layers above. Matches happen every day. Instead of asking for updates each morning, you set a loop: every hour, check for new results and publish them; when a user reports a bug, reproduce it, fix it, verify the fix. The system maintains itself. You review the outcome, not the keystrokes.

Running that reliably takes a handful of ingredients, and Addy Osmani names six: automation for the schedules and triggers, isolated workspaces so parallel changes never collide, reusable skills, connectors to your tools, subagents that verify the work, and state that survives between runs.

  • AutomationRuns tasks on a schedule or trigger.
  • WorkspacesIsolated, so parallel changes never collide.
  • SkillsReusable know-how the system draws on.
  • ConnectorsLinks the system to your business tools.
  • SubagentsSeparate checks that verify finished work.
  • StateProgress that survives between separate runs.

VERDICTHype, or real?

Partly hype. Nobody has yet shown loop engineering producing something the layers below could not, and an unchecked self-prompting system mostly produces more volume, not more value.

But the trajectory is real, and every layer still matters: a badly written prompt stays badly written inside the smartest loop. What grows is the scope of what you can delegate: from an answer, to a task, to a project, to an operation that runs on its own.

SO WHATWhat this changes for you

You already do prompt engineering every day, perhaps without naming it. The next skill is not typing better requests. It is designing the checks around a system that works without you: deciding what runs on a schedule, what gets verified and by what, and where a human must stay in the loop. That is a product skill more than a technical one. And it can be learnt.

This ladder, from first prompts to flows your team runs on its own, is exactly what I teach.
